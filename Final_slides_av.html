01. The Premise

Separating Signal from Hype

Optimistic forecasts predict imminent, widespread adoption of AVs, drawing parallels to consumer electronics.

"This analysis challenges that premise."

Motor vehicles are durable, highly-regulated goods where system failure is catastrophic. Historical data suggests a far more gradual, multi-decade transition.

Our Objective

To provide a realistic, evidence-based forecast for AV deployment and its strategic implications for transport planning.

03. The Central Tension

Balancing potential gains against significant new externalities.

Benefits

Internal (User)
  • Reduced driver stress
  • Mobility for non-drivers
  • Lower taxi/commercial driver costs
External (Society)
  • Potential for increased safety
  • Increased road capacity
  • Reduced parking demand

Costs & Risks

Internal (User)
  • Increased vehicle costs
  • New risks from system failures
  • Reduced privacy and security
External (Society)
  • Increased infrastructure costs
  • Risks to non-auto travelers
  • Increased VMT leading to more congestion & sprawl

Key Insight: External costs, particularly the risk of increased Vehicle Miles Traveled (VMT), can negate many projected societal benefits if not managed by policy.

06. The Deployment S-Curve

Historical data on automotive technology adoption—from automatic transmissions to airbags—provides the most robust model for forecasting AV deployment.

"50% fleet penetration will not be reached until approximately 2060."

07. Historical Precedent

Technology Deployment Cycle Cost Premium Market Saturation
Automatic Transmissions 50 years (1940s-90s) $1,500 90% U.S.
Air Bags 25 years (1973-98) Hundreds 100% (Mandated)
Electric Vehicles 100+ years $10,000+ Projected 80%+

Implication: Autonomous systems, with significantly higher complexity and cost premiums, will likely follow a similar or longer cycle. 2030 forecasts are inconsistent with history.

08. The Unintended Consequence

Induced Travel Demand (The Rebound Effect)

As cost per mile decreases, travel demand increases. Private electric AVs risk significantly increasing VMT and exacerbating congestion by lowering the barriers to travel.

Strategic Conclusions

1

Adjust Timelines

Planners must operate on a realistic, multi-decade deployment forecast (2040-2060), not industry hype.

2

Policy Over Tech

The most critical variable is not the vehicle itself, but the regulatory and pricing framework around it.

3

Shared First

Actively incentivize shared autonomous vehicles (SAVs) to maximize societal benefit and mitigate VMT growth.

4

Implement TDM Now

Strategies like efficient road pricing are essential to ensure AVs solve, rather than worsen, congestion.
